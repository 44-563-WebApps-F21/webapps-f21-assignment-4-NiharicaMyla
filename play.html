A Woman of No Importance: A Play.

SECOND ACT
SCENE
_Drawing-room at Hunstanton_, _after dinner_, _lamps lit_. _Door_ L.C. _Door_ R.C.

[_Ladies seated on sofas_.]

MRS. ALLONBY.

Certainly not to him. Unless, of course, one wants to grow tired of him.

LADY STUTFIELD.

Oh! . . . yes. I see that. It is very, very helpful. Do you think, Mrs. Allonby, I shall ever meet the Ideal Man? Or are there more than one?

MRS. ALLONBY.

There are just four in London, Lady Stutfield.

LADY HUNSTANTON.

Oh, my dear!

MRS. ALLONBY.

[_Going over to her_.]

What has happened? Do tell me.

LADY HUNSTANTON

[_in a low voice_]

I had completely forgotten that the American young lady has been in the room all the time. I am afraid some of this clever talk may have shocked her a little.

MRS. ALLONBY.

Ah, that will do her so much good!

LADY HUNSTANTON.

Let us hope she didn’t understand much. I think I had better go over and talk to her.

[_Rises and goes across to_ HESTER WORSLEY.]

Well, dear Miss Worsley.

[_Sitting down beside her_.]

How quiet you have been in your nice little corner all this time! I suppose you have been reading a book? There are so many books here in the library.

HESTER.

No, I have been listening to the conversation.

LADY HUNSTANTON.

You mustn’t believe everything that was said, you know, dear.

HESTER.

I didn’t believe any of it.
